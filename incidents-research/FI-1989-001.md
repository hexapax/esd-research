## Sources Found

### CDC MMWR - Electricity-Related Deaths on Lakes, Oklahoma, 1989-1993
- **URL:** https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/00042081.htm
- **Type:** Government epidemiological report (CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report)
- **Key Information:**
  - Documents five electricity-related drowning deaths on Oklahoma lakes from June 1989 through September 1993. All five victims were males aged 13-50 years.
  - **Case 4 matches this incident:** On December 8, 1989, a 32-year-old man and his co-workers were rewiring a water pump submerged in "lake A" (two adjoining lakes in northeastern Oklahoma) and stringing electrical wire from the water pump up a hill to a relay station. When his co-workers were unable to locate him, they searched the area and found blood on rocks along the shoreline. A lake patrol diver found the man under 25 feet of water; he was alive and had a laceration above his right eye. CPR efforts were unsuccessful. The medical examiner listed the cause of death as drowning.
  - "Lake A" is described as two adjoining lakes in northeastern Oklahoma with 4,000 private docks and 120 commercial docks. This is consistent with Grand Lake O' the Cherokees and Lake Hudson, both managed by the Grand River Dam Authority (GRDA).
  - Four of the five deaths (Cases 1-4) occurred at "lake A" in northeastern Oklahoma; one (Case 5) occurred at "lake B" in southern Oklahoma.
  - A 1989 inspection of 116 commercial docks at the two adjoining lakes found 96% violated the National Electrical Code, with ungrounded electrical systems being the most common violation.
  - The report was authored by OSDH (Oklahoma State Department of Health) investigators and CDC's Division of Unintentional Injury Prevention.

## Conflicting Information
- **Fault description discrepancy:** The FI-1989-001 source file (Rev. 8/15/2025 ESDPA list, item #109) describes the cause as "a fault in dock wiring." However, the Mike Holt versions of the Shafer/Rifkin list describe the December 1989 Oklahoma incident as "fault in submersible pump." The CDC MMWR Case 4 (December 8, 1989) describes the victim and co-workers as "rewiring a water pump submerged in lake A and stringing electrical wire from the water pump up a hill to a relay station." The CDC account is consistent with both descriptions -- the work involved a submersible pump AND electrical wiring associated with dock infrastructure. It appears the Rev. 8/15/2025 ESDPA list may have reclassified the fault as "dock wiring" while earlier versions classified it as a "submersible pump" fault. Alternatively, the source document extraction may have confused this incident's description with that of the July 1991 Oklahoma incident (FI-1991-001, item #107), which is consistently described as "fault in dock wiring."
- **Date precision:** The source file states "December 1989" (month only). The CDC MMWR report provides the precise date of December 8, 1989.
- **Cause of death:** The source file and ESDPA list classify this as an "Electric Shock Drowning," but the CDC medical examiner listed the cause of death simply as "drowning" -- not electrocution or electric shock drowning. This contrasts with other cases in the same CDC report where the ME explicitly listed electrocution as the cause (Cases 1, 3, and 5).

## Additional Details
- **Victim:** 32-year-old male. No name provided in any source.
- **Precise date:** December 8, 1989 (from CDC MMWR).
- **Circumstances:** The victim was performing electrical work -- rewiring a submerged water pump and stringing wire to a relay station -- rather than swimming or recreating. This was an occupational incident, not a recreational one.
- **Location:** "Lake A" in the CDC report refers to two adjoining lakes in northeastern Oklahoma. Based on the description (4,000 private docks, 120 commercial docks, managed by a local dam authority), this is almost certainly Grand Lake O' the Cherokees and Lake Hudson, both managed by the Grand River Dam Authority (GRDA).
- **Physical evidence:** Co-workers found blood on rocks along the shoreline, and the victim was found under 25 feet of water with a laceration above his right eye. This suggests he may have been shocked, fell and struck rocks, then entered the water.
- **Rescue attempt:** A lake patrol diver found the man alive underwater, but CPR was unsuccessful.
- **Related incidents at same location:** Three other ESD fatalities occurred at the same "Lake A" between 1989 and 1993 (Cases 1-3 in the CDC report), indicating chronic electrical safety problems at this lake system.
- **Regulatory response:** Following the five deaths, the Oklahoma State Department of Health recommended that the local dam authority require electrical inspection of all commercial docks before issuing permits, terminate electrical service to docks failing inspection, and require regular inspections (every 3 years for commercial, every 2 years for private docks).
