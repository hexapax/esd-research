## Sources Found

### CDC MMWR Report: Electricity-Related Deaths on Lakes -- Oklahoma, 1989-1993
- **URL:** https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/00042081.htm
- **Type:** Government epidemiological report (CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report, published May 31, 1996)
- **Key Information:**
  - From June 1989 through September 1993, five males aged 13-50 died from electricity-related incidents on Oklahoma lakes
  - Four deaths occurred at two adjoining lakes in northeastern Oklahoma (anonymized as "Lake A"), and one at a lake in the southern part of the state ("Lake B")
  - **Case 3 (July 24, 1991)** is the closest match to FI-1991-001: A 50-year-old man was found lying unconscious on a boat dock at Lake A. His son received an electrical shock while attempting to revive him. An "electrical short was detected in the dock's wiring." The medical examiner ruled the cause of death as electrocution.
  - A 1994 inspection of 116 commercial and private docks at Lake A found that 96% violated the National Electrical Code, with ungrounded electrical systems being the most common violation
  - The CDC report anonymized the lake names; "Lake A" in northeastern Oklahoma likely refers to Grand Lake O' the Cherokees and/or Fort Gibson Lake, which are adjoining lakes in that region

## Conflicting Information
- **Date discrepancy:** The raw incident file (FI-1991-001) lists the date as "May 11, 1991," but the Mike Holt newsletter and the OnTime Service list both give the date as "July 1991" for the Oklahoma incident. The CDC MMWR report documents the matching case (Case 3) with a specific date of **July 24, 1991**. The May 11, 1991 date in the source file appears to be erroneous and may have been confused with the date of Item #108 / FI-1991-002 (the Lake Hamilton, Arkansas incident), which occurred on May 11, 1991 according to the Mike Holt list (Item #64). The CDC date of July 24, 1991 is likely more accurate for the Oklahoma incident.
- **Cause description:** The raw incident file describes the cause as "a fault in dock lights had energized the dock frame," while the Mike Holt and OnTime lists describe it more generally as "fault in dock wiring." The CDC report states "an electrical short was detected in the dock's wiring." These are broadly consistent, though the source file is more specific about dock lights being the fault origin and the dock frame being the energized component.
- **Victim found on dock vs. in water:** The CDC Case 3 describes the victim as found "lying unconscious on a dock" (electrocution on the dock itself, not drowning in water), while the source file categorizes this as a "drowning" and it appears in the "Electric Shock Drownings" section. The CDC medical examiner ruled the cause of death as "electrocution," not drowning. This raises a question about whether this was technically an electric shock drowning (victim incapacitated in water) or an on-dock electrocution.

## Additional Details
- **Victim profile:** Per the CDC report, the victim was a 50-year-old male. His son was present and received an electrical shock while attempting to revive his father on the dock.
- **Location context:** The CDC report describes the location as "Lake A" in northeastern Oklahoma. Four of five electricity-related deaths from 1989-1993 occurred at this same lake complex, described as "two adjoining lakes." The most likely identification is Grand Lake O' the Cherokees and Fort Gibson Lake, which are the largest adjoining lakes in northeastern Oklahoma, both managed by the Grand River Dam Authority (GRDA) and the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers.
- **Systemic electrical hazards:** The 1994 Oklahoma State Department of Health inspection of Lake A docks found that 96% of 116 commercial and private docks violated the National Electrical Code. The most common violation was an ungrounded electrical system. This widespread noncompliance provides context for why multiple electrocution deaths occurred at this lake complex over a short period.
- **No victim name identified:** Despite extensive searching, no victim name, specific marina name, or obituary was found. The CDC report anonymized all identifying information, and the incident predates widespread online news archiving (1991).
