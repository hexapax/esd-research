# Research: FI-2005-001 — Spring Lake Electric Shock Drowning (2006)

## Original Incident Summary

Per the ESDPA-derived file, on June 27, 2005, at Summerset Lake near Desoto, St. Louis MO, a teenage boy drowned and two friends were rendered unconscious. The cause was attributed to a utility defect (defective neutral). The original file states: "Teenage boy drowned; two friends rendered unconscious; utility at fault (defective neutral)."

## Corrections Needed

1. **DATE IS COMPLETELY WRONG.** The original file states "June 27, 2005." Multiple independent sources (STLPR 3/19/2006 article, St. Louis Post-Dispatch 3/20/2006 article, Lake Expo, Find A Grave memorial, MoreLaw case record, WeRemember.com, Gray Ritter & Graham case summary) unanimously confirm the incident occurred on **March 18, 2006**. This is approximately 8-9 months later than stated, and in the wrong year. The date should be corrected to "March 18, 2006."

2. **Day of week verification:** March 18, 2006 was a Saturday, which is consistent with the circumstances described (teens hanging out at a friend's house, jumping in the lake from a hot tub). June 27, 2005 was a Monday. The Post-Dispatch article confirms the incident happened "shortly before 7 p.m. Saturday."

3. **Number of survivors is understated.** The original file says "Dead: 1, Shocked/survived: 2" but the actual count was: Dead: 1 (Nicholas Harbison), Shocked/survived: 3 (Morgan Milfeld, Timothy Fitzpatrick, and Joshua McClure). The outcome field should read "Dead: 1, Shocked/survived: 3."

4. **Location name variation.** The lake is referred to as both "Spring Lake" and the subdivision as "Summer Set" or "Summerset." The original file uses "Summerset Lake" which appears to be a conflation of the subdivision name (Summer Set) and the actual body of water (Spring Lake). More precise: "Spring Lake, Summer Set subdivision, De Soto, Missouri." The Post-Dispatch describes it as "Summer Set Lake, a gated subdivision off Highway E about five miles from De Soto."

5. **Description could be more precise.** The original correctly identifies "utility at fault (defective neutral)" which is accurate. However, the more specific cause was: Ameren's 7,200-volt underground power distribution cable had corroded concentric neutrals (only 3 of 10 wires remaining), causing neutral current to flow through the earth and into the lake, concentrating at the bonded metal swim ladder with a lethal voltage gradient of more than 2 V/ft.

6. **The incident was confirmed by legal proceedings.** The 2009 jury verdict (Jerry "Jay" Harbison, et al. v. AmerenUE, Jefferson County Circuit Court, Judge Gary P. Kramer) provides strong independent corroboration beyond news reports. The jury awarded $2.3 million after finding Ameren liable 9-3, rejecting the utility's defense that dock wiring was responsible. Plaintiff's attorney was Maurice Graham of Gray, Ritter & Graham. This was described as the first known U.S. recovery against a utility for stray voltage from a high-voltage line buried in a lake.

7. **Survivor long-term outcomes now known.** Timothy Fitzpatrick suffered periodic seizures, motor loss in legs, and impaired memory. Morgan Milfeld had mild hypoxic brain injury but became a collegiate soccer player with minor educational accommodations. Both were in induced comas for five days at St. John's Mercy Medical Center following the incident.
