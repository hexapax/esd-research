# Research: FI-1993-002

## Original Incident Summary
A single fatal drowning occurred in Oklahoma in approximately August 1993. A fault in a submersible pump was identified as the cause. The incident took place in a fresh water environment. The original source is the ESDPA ESD Incident List (PDF Item #105), compiled by James D. Shafer and Capt. David E. Rifkin, Quality Marine Services, LLC (Rev. 8/15/2025). Only the month and year are given; no victim name, age, specific lake, or other identifying details appear in the source file.

## Sources Found

### Source 1
**URL:** https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/preview/mmwrhtml/00042081.htm
**Title:** Electricity-Related Deaths on Lakes -- Oklahoma, 1989-1993 (CDC MMWR, Vol. 43 / No. 49, December 16, 1994)
**Relevant Information:**
- This CDC Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report documents five electricity-related deaths on Oklahoma lakes between June 1989 and September 1993.
- **Case 1** in the report is a strong match for FI-1993-002: a 46-year-old male who died on **September 5, 1993** at "Lake A" (an anonymized lake in northeastern Oklahoma) after contacting a submersible (submergible) water pump while scuba diving.
- A witness reported seeing "a blue softball-size ball of flame emitting from a pipe" containing power cables near the submersible pump, indicating exposed and energized wiring.
- The medical examiner ruled the cause of death as **electrocution** (not drowning).
- "Lake A" refers to two adjoining lakes in northeastern Oklahoma. Four of the five deaths in the 1989-1993 period occurred at this lake system.
- A 1989 inspection of 116 commercial docks at "Lake A" found that 96% were not in compliance with the National Electrical Code (NEC); the most common violation was an ungrounded (open ground) system.
- A follow-up inspection in January 1994 by the Oklahoma State Department of Health of 11 commercial docks and 5 private docks found that 96% were still not in NEC compliance.
- The report recommended requiring electrical inspections of all commercial docks before permit issuance, terminating electrical service to non-compliant docks, and mandating periodic re-inspections (every 3 years for commercial docks, every 2 years for private docks).

## Additional Details Not in Original File
- The victim was a **46-year-old male** who was **scuba diving** at the time of the incident.
- The specific date was **September 5, 1993**, not August 1993 as listed in the ESDPA source.
- The location was "Lake A" in **northeastern Oklahoma** (two adjoining lakes; the CDC report anonymizes the lake name). Northeastern Oklahoma lakes popular for scuba diving include Lake Tenkiller Ferry and Grand Lake o' the Cherokees, though the specific identity is not confirmed in any available source.
- A witness observed "a blue softball-size ball of flame emitting from a pipe" containing power cables near the submersible water pump, indicating a dramatic and visible electrical fault.
- The medical examiner ruled the cause of death as **electrocution**, not drowning. This is significant because the original file classifies it as a "drowning" under the "Electric Shock Drownings" section.
- This incident was part of a **cluster of five electricity-related deaths** at Oklahoma lakes over a four-year period (June 1989 through September 1993). Four of the five occurred at the same lake system ("Lake A"), indicating a severe systemic electrical hazard at that location.
- The related incidents at "Lake A" include:
  - **Case 2 (July 27, 1993):** A 13-year-old boy jumped from a dock to swim; immediately surfaced screaming, then submerged. Cause: short circuit in dock lighting wiring contacting metal frame. Cause of death: drowning, possibly secondary to electrical shock.
  - **Case 3 (July 24, 1991):** A 50-year-old male found unconscious on a dock while wearing wet footwear. Cause: electrical short in dock wiring. Cause of death: electrocution.
  - **Case 4 (December 8, 1989):** A 32-year-old male found 25 feet underwater with a head laceration while rewiring a submerged water pump and stringing electrical wire. Cause of death: drowning.
- The widespread NEC non-compliance (96% of docks in both 1989 and 1994 inspections) demonstrates the systemic nature of the electrical hazard at this lake and the failure to remediate despite multiple fatalities.

## Conflicting Information
- **Date discrepancy:** The ESDPA source document (FI-1993-002) lists the date as "August 1993," while the CDC MMWR report gives the specific date as **September 5, 1993**, and the Mike Holt incident lists also record the month as September 1993. The CDC report is based on medical examiner records and is the more authoritative source for the exact date. The "August 1993" date in the ESDPA list appears to be an error.
- **Cause of death terminology:** The original file classifies this under "Electric Shock Drownings" and describes it as a "drowning." However, the CDC medical examiner ruling was **electrocution**, not drowning. The distinction is medically and legally significant -- the victim was killed by the electrical shock itself rather than by subsequent submersion and water aspiration.

## Suggested Corrections
- **Date:** Change from "August 1993" to "September 1993" (or more precisely, "September 5, 1993") based on the CDC MMWR report, which cites medical examiner records.
- **Description enhancement:** The description could be expanded to note that the victim was a 46-year-old male scuba diver who contacted an energized submersible water pump with exposed power cables while diving, and that the medical examiner ruled the cause of death as electrocution rather than drowning.
- **Classification note:** Consider noting that this incident was part of a cluster of five electricity-related deaths at Oklahoma lakes (1989-1993) that triggered a CDC MMWR investigation, and that 96% of commercial docks at the lake were found to be in violation of the National Electrical Code.
