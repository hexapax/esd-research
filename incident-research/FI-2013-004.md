## Corrections Needed

1. **DATE IS WRONG.** The original file states "July 17, 2013" but independent sources unanimously confirm the incident occurred on **July 19, 2013** (Friday evening, approximately 7:15 p.m.). This is a 2-day discrepancy. Correct date: **July 19, 2013**.

2. **Victim identification missing.** The original provides no name. The victim was **Paige Scheel**, age 12, of Syracuse, NY. Born March 27, 2001. Recently graduated from Webster Elementary School.

3. **Location missing specifics.** The original just says "Syracuse NY." Specific location: Second North Street, Syracuse, NY (residential backyard). Note: Sources vary on the exact house number (102, 103, or 202 Second North St.), likely due to reporting errors.

4. **Circumstances need clarification.** The original says "fell in holding extension cord" but sources indicate she was "filling an inflatable pool" and "either picked up an extension cord or electric pump that was plugged into an outlet." It is not entirely clear from available sources whether she was:
   - Holding an extension cord that fell into the water with her
   - Touching a plugged-in electric pump
   - Making contact with an energized extension cord while in or near the water

   The original description oversimplifies what appears to be a more complex scenario involving pool filling equipment.

5. **Witness/survivor detail missing.** A friend was with Paige and tried to pull her out but received an electrical shock. The friend was not seriously injured but this near-miss is significant context. Syracuse Fire Captain David Munski publicly warned about the dangers of attempting rescue near electrically charged water.

6. **Response details missing.** Emergency responders (Rural Metro and Syracuse Fire) found her unresponsive and transported her to Upstate University Hospital where she was pronounced dead.

7. **CPSC involvement.** Scott Wolfson, communications director of the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission, publicly commented on the case, encouraging adult supervision during inflatable pool setup and use.

8. **Electrical safety context.** The incident highlights the extreme danger of using electrical equipment (extension cords, electric pumps, etc.) around water, especially with children's inflatable pools. The immediate "shaking" reaction suggests direct contact with an energized conductor, likely at full household voltage (120V). Sources note the outdoor receptacle was likely not protected by an operable GFCI as required by the National Electrical Code.
