# Research: FI-1993-001

## Original Incident Summary
A single drowning fatality in Texas in September 1993. A fault in a boatlift was identified. The incident occurred in a salt water environment. The source section is "Electric Shock Drownings" and the PDF item number is #104. Minimal details are available; the date is approximate (month only).

## Sources Found

### Source 1
**URL:** https://americanboating.com/electric-shock-drowning/
**Title:** Electric Shock Drowning - American Boating
**Relevant Information:**
- Explains that salt water is 50 to 1,000 times more conductive than fresh water.
- In salt water, the human body only slows electricity slightly, so most current goes around a swimmer rather than through them.
- In salt water, the current required to cause ESD would be so great that current-limiting circuit breakers would trip first, providing protection.
- However, it is "impossible to rule out the potential for ESD or electrocution in seawater provided the current flow is high enough."

## Additional Details Not in Original File
- ESD researchers note that salt water's higher conductivity means the current required to cause ESD would typically trip circuit breakers before reaching dangerous levels for swimmers. This raises questions about whether this incident was truly an ESD event.

## Conflicting Information
- **Salt water vs. ESD classification:** The incident is listed under "Electric Shock Drownings," yet ESD science indicates that ESD is not expected to occur in salt water under normal fault current conditions (per American Boating). The lack of a confirmed cause of death makes it unclear whether this was truly an ESD event or a drowning coincidentally associated with a boatlift electrical fault.
