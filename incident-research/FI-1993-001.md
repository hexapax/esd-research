# Research: FI-1993-001

## Original Incident Summary
A single drowning fatality in Texas in September 1993. A fault in a boatlift was identified. The incident occurred in a salt water environment. The source section is "Electric Shock Drownings" and the PDF item number is #104. Minimal details are available; the date is approximate (month only).

### Source 5
**URL:** https://americanboating.com/electric-shock-drowning/
**Title:** Electric Shock Drowning - American Boating
**Relevant Information:**
- Explains that salt water is 50 to 1,000 times more conductive than fresh water.
- In salt water, the human body only slows electricity slightly, so most current goes around a swimmer rather than through them.
- In salt water, the current required to cause ESD would be so great that current-limiting circuit breakers would trip first, providing protection.
- However, it is "impossible to rule out the potential for ESD or electrocution in seawater provided the current flow is high enough."

## Additional Details Not in Original File
- The cause of death for this incident was listed as "not available" in all source versions, meaning the drowning was never conclusively attributed to electric shock.
- Earlier versions of the Shafer/Rifkin list date this incident as "Approx. 1994" rather than "September 1993," suggesting the date was refined or changed between list revisions.
- This appears to be the only salt water fatality on the entire ESD incident list. ESD researchers have explicitly stated that their studies show ESD cannot occur in salt water under normal fault current conditions, making this incident's classification as an ESD case questionable.
- The item number changed from #60 in older list versions to #104 in the Rev. 8/15/2025 version, likely due to the addition of many newly discovered earlier incidents in subsequent revisions.

## Conflicting Information
- **Date discrepancy:** The original file states "September 1993," but earlier publicly available versions of the same Shafer/Rifkin list (via Mike Holt newsletters) date this incident as "Approx. 1994." The month "September" does not appear in the older versions. It is possible the Rev. 8/15/2025 version refined the date, or the original file may have an error.
- **Item number discrepancy:** The original file cites PDF item #104, while older versions of the list show this as item #60. This is not necessarily a contradiction but reflects list expansion over time.
- **Salt water vs. ESD classification:** The incident is listed under "Electric Shock Drownings," yet ESD researchers (including the authors of the source list) state that ESD cannot occur in salt water under normal conditions. The cause of death being "not available" further complicates whether this was truly an ESD event or a drowning coincidentally associated with a boatlift electrical fault.
