# Research: FI-2000-001

## Original Incident Summary
The original file describes an incident at Norris Lake, Lafollette, Tennessee, dated "2000 or 2001." Two teenage boys were behind a houseboat at Norris Lake when one could not be resuscitated. A damaged power cable was found. The year is uncertain and was filed under 2000 as a default.

## Sources Found

### Source 1
**URL:** https://www.mikeholt.com/newsletters.php?action=display&letterID=1442
**Title:** Electric Shock Drowning Incidents (Mike Holt Enterprises)
**Relevant Information:**
- Date is listed as April 10, 2001 at Norris Lake, Lafollette, Tennessee
- Two teenage boys were swimming behind a houseboat
- One boy climbed onto the swim platform "complaining of feeling severe shock"
- The other boy "fell back from ladder" with his head not below water, suggesting ventricular fibrillation rather than drowning
- The boy who fell from the ladder could not be resuscitated
- Cause: damaged power cable to the boat; the black lead energized the hull; the ground wire was burned in two; the breaker did not trip due to incorrect connection
- The source notes "(may not be exact sequence)" indicating some uncertainty about the precise order of events

## Additional Details Not in Original File
- **Specific date:** The incident occurred on April 10, 2001, resolving the "2000 or 2001" uncertainty in the original file in favor of 2001
- **Mechanism of death:** The second boy's head was reported as not being submerged, suggesting death by ventricular fibrillation (electrocution) rather than drowning. This is significant because the incident is classified as an electric shock drowning but may technically have been an electrocution
- **Detailed electrical fault:** The black (hot) lead of the power cable energized the hull of the houseboat. The ground wire had burned in two (was severed/failed). The circuit breaker did not trip because it was incorrectly connected
- **Sequence details:** One boy climbed onto the swim platform and complained of feeling severe shock. The other boy fell back from a ladder. The source notes the sequence "may not be exact"
- **Both boys affected:** While the original file says "one of two teenage boys" could not be resuscitated (implying the other survived), the more detailed sources confirm both boys were affected by the electrical shock, though only one death is recorded

## Conflicting Information
- **Date:** The original file lists "2000 or 2001" while all external sources consistently give April 10, 2001 as the specific date
- **Number of deaths:** The original file states "Dead: 1" and says one of the two boys could not be resuscitated. The detailed sources are consistent with this -- one boy died (could not be resuscitated) while the other boy, though shocked, apparently survived. There is no direct conflict here, but the original file's phrasing could be clearer about what happened to the second boy

## Suggested Corrections
- **Date should be updated** from "2000 or 2001" to "April 10, 2001" based on consistent sourcing from the Shafer/Rifkin ESD incident list and all derivative sources
- **File naming:** Since the date is confirmed as 2001, the file might be more accurately named FI-2001-001 rather than FI-2000-001, though changing the identifier could cause reference issues
- **Description could be expanded** to note that the cause of death was likely ventricular fibrillation (not submersion drowning), as the victim's head was reported above water
- **Electrical fault details could be added:** The damaged power cable specifically had its black (hot) lead energizing the hull, the ground wire burned through, and the breaker failed to trip due to incorrect wiring -- this is more specific than just "a damaged power cable was found"

## Research Notes
- No independent news articles from 2001 were found covering this specific incident. All sources trace back to the Shafer/Rifkin ESD incident list maintained by Quality Marine Services, LLC and the Electric Shock Drowning Prevention Association
- The lack of independent contemporaneous news coverage is notable but not unusual for incidents from this era, particularly those that predated widespread online news archiving
- This incident should not be confused with the July 4, 2012 Cherokee Lake incident (Noah Dean Winstead, 10, and Nate Lynam, 11) which occurred at a different Tennessee lake and led to Tennessee's "Noah Dean and Nate Act"
