- **Type**: Major regional newspaper (Lexington Herald-Leader)

- **Key info**: **Kevin Short, 34, of Lily, KY (Laurel County)** died Sunday (June 30, 2013) after the aluminum ladder he was using to climb out of the lake at a private dock came into contact with a frayed electrical cord. Officer Chris King of the Kentucky Department of Fish and Wildlife Resources was the investigating officer. Short was vacationing with his family (wife Linda and three children) at a private dock in the Garland Bend area on the south fork of Lake Cumberland. The owner had run an extension cord from a nearby house to a light at the dock. As Short stepped on the ladder to climb out, his weight pushed the ladder down onto the cord (which had a nick in it). Short's son was in the water with him. Short was able to hand his son up to his wife on the dock before he was overcome. **Other family members tried to pull Short away from the ladder but received shocks as well** (confirming the "rescuers felt shocks" detail in the SUSP file). Accident happened about 3:30 p.m.
- **Additional detail**: It was King's understanding that the Army Corps of Engineers had inspected the dock the prior year and that the electrical set-up was in compliance at the time of inspection.

## ESD Probability Assessment

**HIGH — CONFIRMED ELECTROCUTION (with a nuance)**

Official coroner ruling: **electrocution** (cardiac arrest from electrical current). This is technically a direct electrocution death (not an ESD/drowning), as Short's cause of death was cardiac arrest from the electrical current, not drowning. However, the mechanism (voltage-charged dock ladder in contact with swimmer) is the same mechanism as ESD, and the "chain rescue" shock pattern (family members also shocked) is a classic ESD/electrocution-in-water signature. The AuGres Museum catalogs this as an ESD-type incident. Independent sources consistently confirm the electrical cause.

**Note for classification**: This incident may be better classified as dock electrocution (ventricular fibrillation while exiting water) rather than classic ESD (paralysis leading to drowning). The victim reached up to grab the ladder to exit, was immediately electrocuted, and could not be resuscitated. Whether Short's head went underwater is not described in available sources.
