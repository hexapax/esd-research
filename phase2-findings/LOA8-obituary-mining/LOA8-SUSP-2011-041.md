# LOA8-SUSP-2011-041 — Obituary & Source Research

## Target Summary

On August 15, 2011, Michael Scott Knudsen, 18, of Mancelona, Michigan, died by electric shock drowning at the Duncan L. Clinch Park Marina (F-Dock) in West Grand Traverse Bay, Traverse City, Michigan. He jumped from the dock into the water, surfaced screaming he could not swim, and was incapacitated by electrical current leaking into the water from corroded dock wiring. His friend Zachary Kott-Millard jumped in to help and screamed "Current. There's current in the water." Bystander Holly Sellers felt the current when she grabbed Knudsen. Friends on the dock were shocked when they grabbed Knudsen's arms. Paramedics removed Knudsen from the water after electricity was shut off; he was pronounced dead at 8:00 p.m. at a local hospital. Autopsy: "asphyxia by drowning due to low voltage electrocution." A $50 million lawsuit was filed by attorney Geoffrey Fieger. The city was dismissed due to governmental immunity. The family never received any compensation.

This incident corresponds to existing records: FI-2011-002 (ESDPA list, with incorrect date of May 28, 2011), SUSP-2011-041 (LOA2 misclassified drownings file), and LEGAL-2011-001 (LOA3 legal records).

## Conflicting Information

### 1. Date Discrepancy (RESOLVED)
- **FI-2011-002 (ESDPA):** May 28, 2011

- **Resolution:** The ESDPA date of May 28, 2011 is definitively wrong. The correct date is August 15, 2011, confirmed by the obituary (Mortensen Funeral Homes), the AP wire report (CBS Detroit, August 16, 2011), Interlochen Public Radio's same-day coverage (August 16, 2011), multiple Michigan Court of Appeals opinions, and every other independent source.

### 2. Full Name Spelling
- **Obituary and court records:** Michael S. Knudsen (middle name not spelled out)
- **News sources consistently:** Michael Scott Knudsen
- **Interlochen Public Radio initial report:** "Michael Knudson" (misspelling in early report)
- **Resolution:** Full name is Michael Scott Knudsen. The Interlochen Public Radio misspelling ("Knudson") appears only in the August 16, 2011 initial report and was corrected in all subsequent coverage.

### 3. No-Swimming Signs
- **Plaintiff's position (attorney Dean Robb):** "There was no no-swimming sign"
- **City's position:** Swimming was prohibited; signs were posted
- **Global Marine Insurance:** States "no swimming signs posted inside the marina breakwall"

- **Resolution:** Disputed. The truth appears to be that swimming was technically prohibited but commonly done and not strictly enforced. The Traverse City LipDub 2011 video (recorded less than a month before the death) showed people swimming at the marina.

### 4. Fieger's Contradictory Statements About Cause of Death
- **Fieger press release (September 2011):** Blamed electrified water from negligent municipal wiring
- **Fieger hearing statement (later):** "Knudsen did not die because of an electric current, but instead he died because he was using drugs"
- **Autopsy (medical examiner):** "Asphyxia by drowning due to low voltage electrocution"
- **Resolution:** The Michigan Court of Appeals (2023 opinion) noted this contradiction was relevant to Fieger's credibility. The autopsy finding of "low voltage electrocution" is the authoritative medical determination.

### 5. Zachary Kott-Millard's Age and Residence

## Recommended Follow-up

1. **No further research needed on this specific incident.** The incident is extremely well-documented across obituary, 20+ news sources, 5 Michigan Court of Appeals opinions, insurance industry publications, and marine trade press.

2. **FI-2011-002 date correction should be finalized.** The ESDPA date of May 28, 2011 is wrong; the correct date is August 15, 2011. This has already been documented in existing research files.

3. **The Fieger "drug use" claim is unsubstantiated.** During a hearing, Fieger claimed Knudsen "did not die because of an electric current, but instead he died because he was using drugs." The autopsy conclusively states "asphyxia by drowning due to low voltage electrocution." Fieger's contradictory statement was made in the context of a legal malpractice defense (arguing the underlying case had no merit). No toxicology evidence supporting drug use has been found in any public source. The Michigan Court of Appeals noted this contradiction as relevant to Fieger's credibility.

4. **The broader ESD safety impact is notable.** Multiple sources confirm this case "spurred marinas across the state to make certain their electrical systems were up to code." This was a direct policy outcome despite the legal case being dismissed.

5. **Michigan Court of Appeals cases COA 304695 and COA 274266** (referenced in project memory as additional MI ESD court cases at Horseshoe Lake and Warner v. Scavo) are separate incidents and should be researched independently.
