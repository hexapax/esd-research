## What Happened
While enjoying Memorial Day weather on the sundeck of their moored houseboat at a lake resort in the Southeast United States, a mother and her adult daughter decided to go for a swim to cool off. A second daughter was about to follow them when she looked down and saw her mother floating face down near the swim ladder. Her sister was nowhere to be seen. Attempts to resuscitate the mother failed despite CPR by a witness. Rescuers found the daughter more than 50 feet below the surface, too late to save her.

The post mortem suggested both had drowned because "neither body had suffered any physical trauma." The surviving daughter reported that both women were "good swimmers." No explanation was initially apparent.

Investigation revealed the cause: that morning, the boat owner (husband and father of the victims) had been converting the shore power cord to work with newly installed marina power pedestals. In making the conversion, he miswired the ground and hot wire at the female plug connector, energizing the boat's aluminum hull, railing, and ladder. Additionally, the boat's internal wiring had been modified and the incoming power changed from 125V to 125/250V, exceeding the rated capacity.

This energized the underwater metal hull, creating an electrical field in the freshwater marina. Both women, being good swimmers with no history of medical problems, were incapacitated by the field and drowned — the classic ESD mechanism.

## ESD Indicators Present
- **STRONGEST — Multiple simultaneous victims at same dock/boat**: Both women entered the water together from the same houseboat and both died — the canonical multi-victim ESD signature.
- **STRONGEST — "No physical trauma" at autopsy**: Post mortem explicitly stated no physical trauma found, yet both women drowned. This is the forensic absence-of-injury pattern characteristic of ESD.
- **STRONGEST — Exceptional swimmers**: Both women were described as "good swimmers" by the surviving daughter — the family member's observation that drowning made no sense.
- **MODERATE — Near boat connected to shore power**: The houseboat was plugged into newly installed marina shore power pedestals at the time of death.
- **MODERATE — Electrical fault confirmed by investigation**: The boat owner had miswired the shore power connection that morning, energizing the aluminum hull and ladder. Cause was definitively established through electrical investigation.
- **MODERATE — Freshwater marina**: Event occurred in a freshwater lake marina, the specific environment where ESD is dangerous (saltwater would not support this mechanism).

## Notes
This case was published as a case study in an electrical trade magazine in April 2004, deliberately anonymizing the location and victims' identities. The case is used as a training example for the ESD phenomenon. The surviving daughter's testimony — that both women were "good swimmers" and that the post mortem found no trauma — is the most powerful ESD indicator. The case was initially categorized as drowning because the autopsy found nothing to indicate another cause until the electrical investigation established the miswired shore power connection. This is one of the clearest examples of a case initially filed as drowning that was later determined to be ESD through electrical investigation rather than forensic finding. The date is approximated as 2004 based on the publication date of "The Case of the Hot Marina" article.
