# SUSP-2015-054: One Dead, One Saved — Lake of the Ozarks, Missouri — June 21, 2015 — Two Swimmers "Felt Electricity in the Water"; Power Cut Saved Second Victim

## Classification
- **ESD Probability**: HIGH
- **Incident Type**: Fatal drowning; two swimmers felt electricity in water; cutting power saved second swimmer
- **File Number**: SUSP-2015-054
- **LOA Task**: LOA2-C

---

## Incident Summary

On June 21, 2015, two swimmers at Lake of the Ozarks, Missouri, both "felt electricity in the water." One drowned after grabbing a ladder; the second survived after someone cut power to the dock, eliminating the electrical hazard. The act of cutting power and observing the second swimmer's recovery confirms that the electricity in the water was the cause of incapacitation — a natural experiment that proves ESD causation.

## HIGH-Probability ESD Indicators

### Indicator A: Two Persons Simultaneously "Felt Electricity in the Water"
Both swimmers reported feeling electricity in the water. Two individuals simultaneously experiencing the same electrical sensation in the same body of water is a defining ESD indicator — electricity in the water affects all persons within the voltage gradient simultaneously.

### Indicator B: Cutting Power Eliminated the Hazard and Saved Second Swimmer
When someone cut power to the dock, the electrical hazard in the water was eliminated, and the second swimmer was able to be rescued. This is a definitive natural experiment: the removal of the electrical source directly preceded the victim's ability to be rescued. This proves that electricity in the water — not a physiological event intrinsic to the swimmer — was the cause of incapacitation.

### Indicator C: First Victim Drowned Grabbing Ladder — Consistent with ESD Paralysis at Ladder
The fatal victim drowned after grabbing a ladder. Ladders are a common ESD hazard point: current flows through the ladder from the boat/dock wiring into the water. A swimmer grabbing an energized ladder in water receives the full shock voltage between the ladder contact point and the surrounding water, potentially receiving a higher dose than swimmers who are merely immersed in the voltage gradient.
