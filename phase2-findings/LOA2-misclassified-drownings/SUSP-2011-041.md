## What Happened

On August 15, 2011, 18-year-old Michael Knudsen and friends jumped from the F dock at Clinch Park Marina into Grand Traverse Bay. Knudsen surfaced screaming that he could not swim. Zachary Kott-Millard immediately jumped in to help — and began screaming "Current. There's current in the water." A bystander, Holly Sellers, began to help Knudsen but when she grabbed him "the current began to run through her as well." Friends on the dock tried to lift Knudsen from the water and received electrical shocks. Eventually a friend on the dock was able to pull Kott-Millard out of the water. Paramedics removed Knudsen after the electricity was turned off. He was taken to hospital where he was pronounced dead at 8:00 p.m. Autopsy: "asphyxia by drowning due to low voltage electrocution."

A subsequent investigation by Windemuller Electric found: "corroded, broken, and over-heated equipment, ground materials and an ungrounded conductor that had worn through its insulation making contact with the junction box." City documents showed two separate breaks in a power line had leaked electricity into the water. The cause was traced to deteriorating wiring within junction boxes on the floating dock — metal junction boxes instead of required fiberglass, leading to corrosion. A breaker had failed to respond.

A $50 million lawsuit was filed by Knudsen's family against the city, marina, and contractors. Michigan courts ultimately dismissed the city due to governmental immunity. Evidence indicated the marina had received prior warnings: two swimmers had felt electricity near F dock that same summer; a boater had reported feeling electricity near F dock about a year earlier; two witnesses saw arcing lights and heard buzzing at the neighboring E dock the day before and did not report it. The "No Swimming" sign dispute was contested — plaintiffs argued none was posted; defendants claimed swimming was prohibited.

## ESD Indicators

- Kott-Millard verbally identified electricity in the water in real time: "Current. There's current in the water"
- Holly Sellers felt current pass through her body when she grabbed victim
- Friends on dock felt shocks when grabbing Knudsen's arms
- Prior reports from swimmers feeling electricity at the same dock location the same summer — same zone, multiple witnesses over multiple days
- Autopsy confirmed electrocution as cause
- Post-accident testing confirmed voltage in water from corroded dock wiring

## Electrical Investigation Outcome

Windemuller Electric post-accident inspection found corroded, broken, and overheated junction box equipment with an ungrounded conductor worn through its insulation. Two separate breaks in the underwater power line confirmed. The city was ultimately shielded from liability by governmental immunity. The marina harbormaster's case was the subject of lengthy litigation. The case prompted marina inspections across Michigan. Geoffrey Fieger filed a $50 million suit; the underlying negligence suit was ultimately dismissed; a subsequent legal malpractice suit against Fieger was pending before the Michigan Court of Appeals as of 2018.
