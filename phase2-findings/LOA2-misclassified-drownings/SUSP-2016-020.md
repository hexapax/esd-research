## What Happened

On the evening of June 17, 2016, Donna Berger, Randy Freeney, and their families were boating on Chickamauga Lake near Sale Creek, Tennessee. The group anchored to swim. Berger's 13-year-old son Zachary began struggling in the water. Berger — described by her sister Annette Goodman as an "exceptional swimmer" — jumped in to help him.

Zachary was safely returned to the boat. However, witnesses then noticed Berger was still in the water and "didn't look right." When asked if she was okay, she shook her head no and dropped her head into the water. At that moment, Randy Freeney — 74, described as a "strong swimmer" — entered the water to assist her. According to Goodman, "this gentleman was swimming and literally just stopped and rolled over on his side. And he was right next to Donna."

Shore residents on Lee Pike observed the distress and called 911. They also entered the water with life vests to assist. The Soddy Daisy Fire Department, Hamilton County Fire Department, and Tennessee Wildlife Resources Agency (TWRA) responded at approximately 7:25 p.m. Both Berger and Freeney were brought to shore, where they were pronounced dead at the scene. Neither was wearing a personal flotation device.

The families were reportedly on a church-related boating trip (both were members of Lake Drive Baptist Church). Berger's sister Annette Goodman, of Charlotte, NC, subsequently gave interviews stating she was convinced both deaths were caused by electric shock drowning. WSOCTV Charlotte's investigative segment (9 Investigates, published approximately July 3, 2016) covered the case in the context of ESD awareness. Randy Freeney's wife, Gloria Freeney, was on the boat with them when they died.

## ESD Indicators Present

- **STRONGEST — Skilled swimmer entered distress with no explanation**: Donna Berger was an "exceptional swimmer" who entered calm, anchored lake water and became incapacitated with no visible explanation (no rip current, no waves, calm freshwater).
- **STRONGEST — Rescuer drowned immediately upon entering water**: Randy Freeney, a "strong swimmer," entered the water and "literally just stopped and rolled over on his side" — the classic ESD rescuer-paralysis pattern with rapid incapacitation.
- **STRONGEST — Both victims incapacitated in same water location**: Two experienced swimmers incapacitated in the same area simultaneously at a freshwater lake during boating season.
- **MODERATE — Freshwater lake environment during boating activity**: Chickamauga Lake is a TVA reservoir, popular with boating. The group anchored among other boaters, making proximity to electrically-faulted vessels plausible.
- **MODERATE — No post-mortem electrical evidence expected**: ESD leaves no physical marks distinguishable from drowning; the "drowning" ruling by TWRA is consistent with how ESD is typically misclassified.
- **MODERATE — Family confident ESD was cause**: Sister Annette Goodman stated to WSOCTV she was "convinced" both deaths were ESD, consistent with witnesses observing the rapid paralysis-like incapacitation.

## Follow-up Electrical Investigation

No electrical investigation of the surrounding boats or water is documented in available sources. TWRA classified the deaths as drownings. No mention of water-current testing, marine electrician involvement, or inspection of nearby vessels has been found in news coverage or public records. The ESDPA listed this incident in their August 2016 update with incomplete details and noted "exact time and location are unknown" — indicating no systematic investigation was completed.

## Notes

The ESDPA has listed this as a likely ESD event but with incorrect date (July 3 vs. June 17) and no specific location. The WSOCTV investigative report used this case as a primary example of suspected ESD. The simultaneous incapacitation of an "exceptional swimmer" followed immediately by the rapid paralysis and drowning of a "strong swimmer" rescuer matches the classic ESD double-fatality pattern documented in confirmed cases (e.g., Cherokee Lake 2012, Lake Tuscaloosa 2017). The water was calm, anchored, freshwater — no current or weather factors identified.

**Recommended follow-up**: Verify whether Hamilton County ME investigated electrical causation; FOIA TWRA incident report; interview survivor witnesses about presence of electrical tingling in water; cross-check which boats were anchored near the incident location and whether any had shore power connections active.
