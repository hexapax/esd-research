## What Happened

In Summer 2017, a male marina owner in his 80s drowned after falling into six-foot-deep water at a boat slip located in a marina on a river in Michigan. The decedent had operated the marina for many years, providing boat maintenance and dock work for slip holders. On the morning of the incident, he left home between 8:00–8:30 a.m. to work at the marina.

When he did not return home at his normal dinnertime, his spouse tried to contact him and, unable to reach him, called a family member who went to the marina. Unable to locate the decedent, emergency response was contacted. The responding sheriff department found him floating upright in the water under a boat slip walkway, with the crown of his head visible above the water. He was not restrained in any way under the water, and the bottom was described as mucky.

The decedent's spouse described him as a "good swimmer." She noted he had previously fallen into the water at least twice before without serious incident (his son was present on prior occasions and assisted him in getting out). On the day of his death, he had been working alone. Tools and keys were found in the ignition of a nearby boat ("Boat A"), suggesting he had been performing maintenance work.

The case was investigated by the Michigan Fatality Assessment and Control Evaluation (MIFACE) program as an occupational fatality. MIFACE personnel interviewed the spouse and visited the marina.

**Critical finding**: Neither the responding sheriff nor the medical examiner investigator checked the water for stray electrical current. The CDC FACE report (17MI208, published March 9, 2020) noted this absence explicitly: "Neither the sheriff or the medical examiner investigator checked the water for stray electrical current."

## ESD Indicators Present

- **STRONGEST — No electrical investigation conducted at marina with shore power**: The marina had active shore power infrastructure (boat slips with electrical service is the standard for a working marina providing boat maintenance). Stray current was never tested for, meaning ESD cannot be ruled out.
- **STRONGEST — Victim found floating upright, not sunk**: Found "floating upright in the water under a boat slip walkway" with "the crown of his head visible." This is atypical for a simple slip-and-fall drowning. ESD victims often remain near the surface or float upright due to the muscle-contraction posture at the time of electrical paralysis.
- **STRONG — "Good swimmer" designation from spouse**: A man described by his spouse as a "good swimmer" drowned in 6 feet of water at his own marina — familiar territory he worked in daily.
- **MODERATE — Working alone on a boat in a marina slip**: The configuration (boat maintenance, shore power connected, freshwater marina on a river) matches all three preconditions for ESD: potential AC source from boat or dock, no witnesses, freshwater environment.
- **MODERATE — Previous falls without drowning**: He had fallen into the water at least twice before without incident. The difference on this occasion — when he was alone and died — could reflect a changed electrical condition (e.g., a new fault on a boat, different power connection).

## Follow-up Electrical Investigation

CDC FACE Report 17MI208 explicitly documents that **no electrical investigation was conducted** — neither the sheriff's office nor the medical examiner checked for stray current in the water. No subsequent testing is mentioned in the report. The MIFACE researchers noted the absence as a gap but the CDC report's recommendations focused on personal flotation devices and safety protocols, not electrical testing. No ESDPA listing found for this case.

## Notes

This case was found through a CDC FACE report, not through press coverage — which means it likely never received public scrutiny as a potential ESD incident. The combination of (1) "good swimmer" found dead in familiar marina water, (2) floating upright in a boat slip, (3) confirmed absence of any electrical investigation, and (4) working marina environment with shore power infrastructure makes this a textbook candidate for ESD misclassification. The victim's prior falls without incident may reflect that no electrical fault existed on those dates, consistent with ESD's intermittent nature.

**Recommended follow-up**: FOIA Michigan state records for Hamilton County ME report and sheriff's incident report; request MIFACE raw data from CDC; contact marina's utility provider for records of power anomalies in Summer 2017; perform or commission current testing of the affected boat slip.
