## Key Technical Content

This is the foundational engineering study on ESD, conducted under a US Coast Guard research grant. The study used controlled field testing to characterize in-water shock hazards around faulty boats:

- **Test methodology**: Used a variable AC voltage source (variac) connected to vessel bonding systems to inject controlled fault current into water under controlled conditions with boats disconnected from shore power. Multiple sites tested with varying water conductivity, temperature, salinity, and geography.
- **Key finding on freshwater**: Freshwater's high resistivity means fault current cannot easily complete its circuit through the water itself — the swimmer's body (lower resistance than fresh water) becomes the preferred current return path. This does not occur in saltwater, where current dissipates through the conductive medium.
- **Fault scenarios covered**: Battery charger faults, wiring insulation failures, submerged junction boxes, shore power cord failures, and bonding system failures
- **Current thresholds from related standards**: 5 mA — muscle control interference; 10–20 mA — let-go threshold; 30 mA — recommended maximum for shore power GFP; 100 mA — original NEC limit (deemed inadequate)
- **Mitigation recommendations**:
  - Install double-pole RCD (30 mA sensitivity, 100 ms trip time) on main vessel AC supply
  - Install 5–10 mA GFCI/RCD on receptacles in galleys, toilets, machinery spaces, and weather decks
  - Whole-boat ELCI (30 mA) at shore power inlet
  - Verify bonding resistance meets ABYC E-11 standards
  - Do not swim near powered boats or docks
- Recommended updates to ABYC standard E-11 for AC/DC systems
- Noted that off-the-shelf devices work if installed correctly; further research needed

## ESD Incidents Cited

The report was motivated by the existing body of ESD incidents documented by Rifkin and Shafer. It does not cite specific incidents within the report text but the authors are the same individuals who maintain the ESD incident database. The 2008 report was specifically commissioned following documented ESD fatalities that the US Coast Guard needed to address.

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

This is the primary government-funded technical study that informed subsequent ABYC E-11 revisions and supported the NEC Article 555 ground-fault protection requirements. The 30 mA threshold for shore power receptacles adopted in NEC 2017 and NFPA 303 traces directly to this study's recommendations. Referenced extensively in IAEI Magazine "Safety in Marinas" and in the NFPA FPRF Marina Risk Reduction project. This report was instrumental in elevating ESD from an anecdotal concern to a technically documented engineering problem.
