## Key Technical Content

Summary of state-level electric shock drowning prevention legislation, which constitutes the primary legal response to ESD in the US (no comprehensive federal legislation has passed as of 2026):

**Tennessee — "Noah Dean and Nate Act" (enacted 2014)**
Named for Noah Dean Winstead (age 10) and Nathan (Nate) Lynam (age 11), who died on July 4, 2012, at Cherokee Lake near Morristown, Tennessee.
Technical requirements:
- GFCIs required in and around boat docks and marinas
- Safety signage required notifying individuals of potential electric shock risks within 100 yards of boat docks
- State Fire Marshal's Office authorized to conduct marina safety inspections
- Marina inspections must occur at least every 5 years
- Ground fault protection required per NEC Article 555 and NFPA 303

**Missouri — "Alexandra and Brayden Anderson Electric Shock Drowning Prevention Act" (SB 622, 2018)**
Named for Alexandra Anderson (age 13) and Brayden Anderson (age 8), killed July 4, 2012.
Requires ground fault protection, inspections, and signage at marinas.

**West Virginia — Michael Cunningham Act**
Named for a specific drowning victim. Requires regular dock inspections, ground fault protection, and posting of warning signs. Codified at W.Va. Code §20-9-3.

**Pennsylvania — Ongoing legislation (reintroduced 2025)**
Sponsored by Senators Jay Costa and Camera Bartolotta; requires ground fault protection, electrical inspections every 5 years, and safety signage at boat docks and marinas. Has been introduced in multiple legislative sessions without passage as of 2025.

**Indiana** — Regulations at Indiana Code Title 14, Article 15, Chapter 13 governing dock electrical safety.

**Federal status**: No comprehensive federal electric shock drowning legislation has been enacted. The Virginia Graeme Baker Pool and Spa Safety Act (2008) addresses suction entrapment hazards but not ESD at marinas. ESD advocates have sought federal action but regulation has remained state-level.

## ESD Incidents Cited

Each state law is named for specific victims:
- Noah Dean Winstead (age 10) and Nathan Lynam (age 11): July 4, 2012, Cherokee Lake, Tennessee

- Michael Cunningham: West Virginia (specific date/location not specified in legislative record)

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

State-level ESD laws have been the primary regulatory response. These laws:
1. Create legal requirements for marina inspections and GFP that go beyond NEC/NFPA code compliance
2. Establish state agency authority (fire marshals, DNR) to enforce electrical safety at marinas
3. Named for specific victims — the naming pattern underscores the connection between individual tragedies and regulatory response
4. Advocate groups (ESDPA, families of victims) have been the primary drivers of state legislation

The absence of federal legislation despite documented fatalities in multiple states is itself a finding — ESD has not achieved the federal regulatory traction of other boating safety hazards.
