## Key Technical Content

Charles Dalziel's work at UC Berkeley from the 1940s through 1970s established the entire scientific foundation for modern electrical shock safety thresholds, including those used in NEC Article 555 and NFPA 303 for ESD prevention:

**Experimental methodology**: Healthy male and female volunteer subjects held copper wire in one hand, placed other hand on brass plate; voltage applied and physiological responses measured.

**Key threshold findings (60 Hz AC, the US power frequency):**
- **1 mA**: Barely perceptible ("let-go" not yet affected)
- **5 mA**: Muscle seizure threshold; interferes with voluntary muscle control, including swimming
- **10–15.87 mA** (men's mean): Let-go threshold — cannot release grip (9.7–21.6 mA range for men)
- **10.5 mA** (women's mean): Let-go threshold for women (lower than men)
- **10–20 mA**: Paralysis of respiratory muscles; risk of respiratory arrest during sustained contact
- **100 mA**: Ventricular fibrillation threshold (if current flows through cardiac region)
- **2 A**: Cardiac standstill and internal organ damage

**Frequency effects**: 50–60 Hz AC is among the most physiologically hazardous frequency bands — more dangerous than DC or high-frequency AC

**GFCI development connection**: A 1971 Underwriters Laboratories study (Smoot) based on Dalziel's work concluded that 81% of home electrocution deaths could have been prevented by GFCI protection. The NEC adopted GFCI recognition in May 1971. The 5 mA trip threshold for Class A GFCIs derives from Dalziel's let-go and muscle-control threshold data.

**"Reevaluation of Lethal Electric Currents" (1968)**: Updated Dalziel's lethal current thresholds, providing more refined data on ventricular fibrillation probability as a function of current magnitude and duration.

## ESD Incidents Cited

Dalziel's work is fundamental research; it does not address ESD specifically. However, his current thresholds are the scientific basis for all ESD prevention standards.

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

Dalziel's experimental data provides the scientific basis for:
- The 5 mA Class A GFCI trip threshold
- The 30 mA GFPE threshold for shore power receptacles in NEC Article 555
- The 100 mA original USCG/NEC threshold
- ABYC E-11 ELCI requirements (30 mA, 100 ms trip)
His work is foundational to every electrical safety standard involving current thresholds near humans.

## Relevance Rating

HIGH — Dalziel's physiological current-threshold data is the scientific foundation for all GFCI, GFPE, ELCI, and RCD trip thresholds used in ESD prevention standards. Any serious ESD research database must include this foundational work as the basis for understanding why specific milliampere thresholds were chosen.
