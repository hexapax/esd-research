## Abstract/Summary
This CDC MMWR report describes five fatalities from electricity-related deaths at Oklahoma lakes between June 1989 and September 1993. All victims were males aged 13-50 years. Four deaths occurred at two adjoining northeastern Oklahoma lakes; one occurred at a southern Oklahoma lake.

Case summaries:
- Case 1 (Sept 5, 1993): 46-year-old scuba diver contacted a submergible water pump; cause: electrocution
- Case 2 (July 27, 1993): 13-year-old jumped from dock to swim (dock lights on); cause: drowning secondary to electrical shock from short wiring
- Case 3 (July 24, 1991): 50-year-old found unconscious on dock with electrical short detected; cause: electrocution
- Case 4 (Dec 8, 1989): 32-year-old rewiring water pump, found underwater after going missing; cause: drowning
- Case 5 (June 1, 1989): 36-year-old installing fuel pump on houseboat, elbow contacted broken light wires connected to 110-volt line; cause: low-voltage electrocution

A 1989 inspection of 116 commercial docks at Lake A found that 96% violated the National Electrical Code, with ungrounded systems as the primary violation.

Recommendations included requiring electrical inspections before issuing dock permits, terminating service to non-compliant docks, mandating inspections every 3 years for commercial and 2 years for private docks, and improving drowning surveillance systems with detailed narrative information.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: Yes — 5 named/dated incidents at Oklahoma lakes 1989-1993, including submersible pump and dock wiring cases
- Prevalence estimates: 5 deaths over ~4 years at two lake areas; 96% of 116 commercial docks at one lake violated NEC
- Forensic markers: Cause of death listed variously as electrocution or "drowning secondary to electrical shock" — demonstrates that ESD was recognized as a distinct category even in 1989-1993
- Key researchers: Oklahoma State Department of Health investigators; CDC surveillance team

## Notes
This is the only CDC MMWR report found that specifically addresses ESD-type deaths at lakes/marinas. It is significant because it documents real incidents with dates and causes, demonstrates the scale of dock electrical code violations (96% non-compliance), and explicitly recommends improving drowning surveillance to capture ESD cases. The note about "improving drowning surveillance systems with detailed narrative information" directly acknowledges the misclassification problem. This report likely prompted awareness in the late 1990s that eventually led to NFPA and ABYC code changes.
