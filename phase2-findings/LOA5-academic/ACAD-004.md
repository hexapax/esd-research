# ACAD-004: Electrocution -- Autopsy Study with Emphasis on "Electrical Petechiae"

| **Journal** | Forensic Science International |
| **Year** | 2002 |
| **PMID/DOI** | PMID: 12062943 / DOI: 10.1016/s0379-0738(02)00061-0 |

## Abstract/Summary
Retrospective autopsy study of 37 electrocution fatalities examining forensic markers. Key findings:
- Electrical burns or current marks were detected in 81% of cases, including all high-voltage electrocutions
- Seven victims lacking burns were electrocuted in wet environments, including six cases of suicide in bathtubs with hair dryers — directly demonstrating that absence of burns does not exclude electrocution in water-related deaths
- Petechial hemorrhages were present in 74% of cases, with characteristic sites being eyelids, conjunctivae, visceral pleura, and epicardium
- Authors propose that "petechiae are not caused by asphyxia but by a combination of venous congestion due to cardiac arrest and a sudden rise in blood pressure"
- "Electrical petechiae" represent a characteristic finding in electrocution regardless of voltage or current pathway, indicating vital origin of events

Published as volume 126(3), pages 210-213. Authors affiliated with Institute of Legal Medicine, University of Munster, Germany.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: Yes — 37 total electrocution cases; 7 wet environment cases without burns (6 bathtub suicides)
- Prevalence estimates: 19% of cases had no electrical burns (all wet environment cases); 74% showed petechiae
- Forensic markers: Electrical burns in 81%; petechial hemorrhages in 74% (eyelids, conjunctivae, visceral pleura, epicardium); absence of burns in all wet/immersion cases; petechiae as secondary indicator in water cases
- Key researchers: B. Karger, O. Suggeler, B. Brinkmann — Institute of Legal Medicine, University of Munster, Germany

## Notes
This study provides the crucial forensic insight that wet environment electrocutions consistently lack external electrical marks — precisely the challenge in diagnosing ESD. The finding that 100% of the wet-environment cases (7/7) had no burns is central to understanding why ESD deaths are so frequently misclassified as regular drowning. The "electrical petechiae" finding — while non-specific — provides a potential secondary marker. B. Brinkmann is the same senior author as ACAD-003, suggesting a productive research program at Munster on electrocution forensics.
