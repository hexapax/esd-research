## Abstract/Summary
Comprehensive review article in the electrical inspection trade journal, describing ESD mechanisms, case examples, and prevention measures. Key content:

Opening case: A party at a marina where a young girl used a swim slide on a newly moored houseboat and immediately experienced shock. A rescuer attempting to help also drowned. Investigation found an AC "hot" lead fault in the lighting circuit to the metal hull, combined with a compromised green safety grounding wire.

Technical findings:
- "As little as 2 v/ft gradient in the water can produce lethal conditions near a faulted boat"
- At 10 milliamps, children may become paralyzed and unable to swim ("let-go current" phenomenon)
- In fresh water with proper bonding, current distribution is approximately 95% through ground wire and 5% through water path
- Salt water distributes current more evenly without creating the same lethal gradients
- Research team "catalogued over 100 similar accidents" available upon request

Technical solutions:
- Marina Guard monitoring system (24-hour ground-fault monitoring with adjustable alarm levels)
- NFPA-303 Code Updates incorporating ground-fault monitoring (2006 edition)
- USCG grant obtained to study appropriate ground-fault protection trip levels

The author advocates for electrical safety seminars targeting non-technical marina staff as the most effective preventive measure.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: Yes — marina opening incident (young girl + rescuer); references catalog of over 100 accidents

## Notes
This 2007 IAEI article is significant as an early comprehensive review of ESD, written when the phenomenon was only beginning to gain attention. The mention of a catalog of 100+ accidents is important — this appears to be an early predecessor to what became the ESDPA's formal incident list. Shafer's Marina Guard system is one of the earliest monitoring technologies for marina ESD hazards. The IAEI (International Association of Electrical Inspectors) is the professional body for electrical code inspectors, making this publication influential for code enforcement and inspection practice. Not peer-reviewed in the academic sense but carries professional authority.
