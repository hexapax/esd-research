## The Misclassification Pipeline

### Step 1: The Incident
- A person enters water near a powered dock, marina, boat lift, or shore-power-connected vessel
- AC current has leaked into the water from faulty wiring or inadequate grounding/bonding
- The victim experiences muscular paralysis — they cannot swim or call for help
- Unless a bystander is also in the water (and feels the current), the victim's distress is indistinguishable from ordinary drowning distress
- The victim drowns

### Step 2: The Discovery
- Victim is found drowned, or witnesses observe a swimmer in distress who then goes under
- First responders arrive — they see a drowning victim
- Standard drowning rescue and resuscitation protocols are applied
- **The water is not tested for electrical current** — this is not standard protocol for drowning responses
- After the victim is recovered, no electrical testing is typically performed at the scene

### Step 3: The Autopsy
- Medical examiner performs standard drowning autopsy
- Cause of death: **drowning** (fluid in lungs, consistent with submersion)
- Electrical injury markers: **absent** — AC current at the levels causing ESD paralysis (10–30 mA) typically does not produce burns, internal tissue damage, or other pathological findings identifiable at autopsy
- AC current (as opposed to DC) is less likely to produce distinctive arborescent burns
- Medical examiner has no basis to add W86 (electrical exposure) to the death certificate
- **Death is coded as drowning** — classification: unintentional drowning, W65–W74

### Step 4: Case Closure
- If no witness reported feeling electrical current in the water, no investigation is triggered
- Local law enforcement may investigate but typically lacks expertise to recognize ESD indicators
- The electrical infrastructure at the dock/marina is not tested
- The fault that caused the death may persist — potentially causing future incidents at the same location
- The case is closed as "drowning" — possibly attributed to alcohol, fatigue, swimming ability, or unknown causes

## Conditions Under Which ESD Is Correctly Identified
ESD is correctly identified only when:
1. A witness was also in the water and felt the electrical shock
2. Multiple victims are involved simultaneously (increasing the probability someone survives to report the sensation)
3. A responder or investigator happens to test the water with specialized equipment
4. The incident occurs at a location with prior ESD history that triggers a heightened investigation
5. A civil lawsuit follows and attorneys engage expert engineers who test the scene

## Quantifying the Gap
- NASBLA states: "relatively few **documented** electric shock drownings occur each year" with emphasis on "documented"
- American Boating Association: "In just four months last summer, there were seven confirmed ESD deaths and at least that many near misses; in all likelihood, dozens more incidents went undetected"
- NFPA: "it is not known exactly how many cases of ESD occur annually in the United States or elsewhere"
- Oklahoma MMWR: The state identified 5 cases through a **multi-source** surveillance system including newspaper clippings, hospital records, and medical examiner records — yet this comprehensive system still likely missed cases

## The Near-Miss Problem
Near-misses are also systematically underreported:
- A swimmer who feels tingling and exits the water may not report it to anyone
- A report to a marina owner may not generate any official record
- BARD requires reports only when there is "an injury or death" — sub-threshold near-miss shocks create no reporting obligation
- The invisible nature of electrical current means near-miss victims often attribute their experience to cramps or anxiety

## Structural Reforms That Would Reduce Misclassification
- Require water electrical testing as part of standard drowning investigation protocol
- Train first responders to recognize ESD indicators (unusual circumstances, location near powered docks)
- Add a dedicated ESD category to BARD and CPSC reporting systems
- Require medical examiners to consider electrical etiology in drowning deaths near powered water infrastructure
