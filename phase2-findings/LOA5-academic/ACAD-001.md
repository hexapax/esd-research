# ACAD-001: Electrically Induced Deaths Involving Water Immersion

## Abstract/Summary
Examines cases where electrical shock combined with water immersion resulted in fatalities. The key forensic finding is that "the cooling effect of the water prevents heating of tissues," eliminating the typical electrical burn indicators that pathologists rely on. The actual cause of death in some cases was determined to be drowning rather than electrocution, despite electrical shock being the initiating factor. The author (from Goodson Engineering, Denton, Texas) emphasizes that thorough site investigation is essential for pathologists conducting autopsies in such cases — both to establish accurate cause of death and to prevent similar incidents at locations where previous fatalities occurred.

Published as volume 14(4), pages 330-333.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: Yes — multiple cases where electrical shock in water led to drowning, not direct electrocution
- Prevalence estimates: None given; focuses on individual cases
- Forensic markers: Water cooling prevents burn marks; death mechanism shifts from electrocution to drowning; site investigation is critical for correct diagnosis
- Key researchers: M.E. Goodson, Goodson Engineering, Denton, Texas

## Notes
This is one of the earliest peer-reviewed papers addressing the specific ESD phenomenon (low-voltage current in water leading to drowning rather than obvious electrocution). The author's engineering firm affiliation (Goodson Engineering) suggests a forensic engineering rather than pure pathology perspective. The paper predates the term "Electric Shock Drowning" but describes the same phenomenon. It is frequently cited in subsequent ESD literature as a foundational reference. The full PDF is available at goodsonengineering.com but is image-based and not text-extractable.
