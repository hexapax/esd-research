## Abstract/Summary
Systematic review defining and examining "drowning incidents not readily recognized or not commonly suspected as a result of human or environmental factors that usually accompany drowning incidents" (DIPUCs). Reviewed 351 papers meeting inclusion criteria. Covers etiologies including environmental, behavioral, physiologic, cardiac, and seizure-related causes, as well as electrical injury.

For electrical injury specifically:
- Noted as "an infrequent cause of DIPUCs" but can occur through in-water electrical current exposure or lightning strikes
- Lightning accounts for approximately 27 deaths per year in the United States, with 8% associated with swimming
- Children represent "almost half" of those receiving electrical shocks in swimming pools
- Diagnostic challenge: "the cooling effect of surrounding water may obscure the usually evident entry and exit points of the electrical current on the victim's body, the differentiation of electrocution from drowning can be difficult for the pathologist"
- Emphasizes autopsy with postmortem molecular genetic testing when DIPUC diagnosis remains unclear

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: No specific named incidents — systematic review
- Prevalence estimates: Lightning-related swimming deaths ~27/year x 8% = ~2 swimming lightning deaths/year (U.S.); ESD frequency not separately quantified
- Forensic markers: Water cooling obscures entry/exit burns; molecular genetic testing recommended when cause unclear
- Key researchers: Duignan KM, Ratzan RM (emergency medicine, likely U.S. academic medical centers)

## Notes
This 2024 review is the most recent peer-reviewed paper found that explicitly addresses ESD within a medical journal context. The framing of ESD as a "DIPUC" — a drowning precipitated by unusual causes — is exactly the conceptual framework needed for understanding why ESD deaths are misclassified: they look like ordinary drownings and require additional investigation to identify the electrical cause. The paper validates the central ESD research challenge. Published in Resuscitation Plus (Elsevier), a peer-reviewed open-access resuscitation journal.
