## Key Technical Content

A chronological account of how ground-fault protection requirements for marinas evolved through successive NEC editions, written by a state labor/industry official who participated in the standards development process:

- **2011 NEC**: First edition with GFPE requirements; initial threshold at 100 mA. Article notes this was an important first step but inadequate for preventing ESD in children
- **2014 NEC**: Technical committee formally acknowledged that 100 mA "far above the Class A GFCI levels of 4–6 mA" was insufficient to prevent muscle tetanization in children, causing drowning; debate over lowering threshold began in earnest
- **2017 NEC**: The 30 mA maximum for protection devices was achieved, with requirement that protection must interrupt power within 100 milliseconds. Hunter characterizes this as the "breakthrough" edition representing a compromise: "sufficient to reduce leakage current while minimizing unwanted tripping"
- **2020 NEC**: "Complete restructuring" of Article 555. Tiered requirements: 30 mA GFPE for shore power receptacles; 4–6 mA GFCI for 15–20A receptacles; 100 mA GFPE for feeder/branch-circuit conductors; leakage measurement devices required when more than three receptacles supply shore power
- **2023 NEC**: Reorganized into feeder (100 mA), branch circuit (30 mA GFPE), and general receptacle (4–6 mA GFCI) tiers; Section 555.15 added for equipment replacement compliance requirements

Author Hunter was a member of the Technical Panel for the 2015 NFPA FPRF Marina Shock Hazard Research Planning Workshop (ACAD-027).

## ESD Incidents Cited

Article references the general body of ESD incidents and child drowning deaths as motivation for code changes, without citing specific named cases.

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

Provides insider perspective on the NEC standards development process from a state regulator who participated in key committee decisions. Validates the timeline of code changes documented in other sources. Published in IAEI Magazine — read by inspectors responsible for enforcing these standards.

## Relevance Rating

HIGH — Authoritative first-person account of the NEC Article 555 evolution from a standards committee participant. Provides the regulatory history from the perspective of those who wrote the standards, explaining the reasoning behind each threshold change.
