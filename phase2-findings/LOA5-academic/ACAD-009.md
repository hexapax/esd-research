## Abstract/Summary
Short review article examining forensic challenges in diagnosing electrocution deaths at autopsy. Published as volume 19(1), pages 91-93.

Key forensic findings:
- Lethal mechanisms include ventricular fibrillation or respiratory paralysis affecting respiratory muscles or brainstem
- Current flow depends on voltage and tissue resistance
- Most deaths are accidental rather than intentional
- Major evaluation problems arise in two scenarios: (1) when electrocution is evident from scene findings but no injuries appear at autopsy, and (2) when electrical burns are found at autopsy without corresponding equipment defects at the death scene
- The first scenario (scene evidence of electrocution, no autopsy findings) directly describes the typical ESD death

Author affiliation: School of Biomedicine, University of Adelaide, and Forensic Science SA, Adelaide, South Australia.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: No — review article
- Prevalence estimates: None
- Forensic markers: Ventricular fibrillation or respiratory paralysis as lethal mechanisms; absence of autopsy findings when electrocution occurs in water; importance of scene investigation
- Key researchers: Roger W. Byard (University of Adelaide/Forensic Science SA) — one of the world's most prolific forensic pathology authors

## Notes
Byard is an internationally recognized forensic pathologist with hundreds of publications. This short review, while not exclusively about ESD, directly addresses the core forensic challenge: when electrocution happens in water, autopsy findings may be completely absent. The paper's acknowledgment of the "no injuries at autopsy" scenario validates the fundamental diagnostic problem that leads to ESD misclassification. Byard also published on suicidal electrocution in bathtubs (J Forensic Sci, 2013, DOI: 10.1111/1556-4029.12163), adding to his relevant work. His institution, Forensic Science SA, conducts forensic pathology for South Australia.
