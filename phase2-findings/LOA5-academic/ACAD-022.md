## Key Technical Content

Comprehensive analysis of 157 fatal electrical accident records in Finland spanning four decades. While not exclusively about ESD, this paper provides critical comparative data:

- Death rates declined dramatically from 0.29 per 100,000 population annually to 0.00–0.07 in the 2010s — attributable primarily to mandatory RCD adoption
- 37% of all fatal accidents could probably have been prevented if circuits had been protected with a residual current device
- Zero ESD incidents in the entire 40-year dataset, despite Finland having abundant freshwater and substantial marina/boating activity
- 76% of fatalities occurred between April–September (warm weather season), consistent with ESD risk patterns in the US
- 92.5% of victims were male; child casualties remained exceptionally rare
- By the 2010s, railway overhead power line contact became the dominant fatal electrical accident type — shifting away from domestic/low-voltage accidents that RCDs address

Technical note: The dramatic drop in domestic electrical fatalities corresponds with progressive adoption of mandatory RCD requirements in Finnish electrical standards, demonstrating the effectiveness of this protective technology.

## ESD Incidents Cited

No ESD incidents found in Finland across 157 fatal electrical accidents over 40 years. This negative finding — absence of ESD in a country with mandatory RCDs and abundant freshwater — is itself a significant data point.

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

This study is cited by Linja-aho's IEEE discussion paper (ACAD-021) to support the argument that mandatory RCD/ELCI requirements effectively eliminate ESD. Provides comparative international evidence for US regulatory discussions about mandatory ELCI requirements under NEC Article 555 and NFPA 303.

## Relevance Rating

HIGH — Provides the empirical underpinning for the international regulatory comparison. The zero-ESD finding in a country with mandatory RCDs is powerful evidence for the preventability of ESD through proper electrical protection standards.
