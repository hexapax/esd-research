## Abstract/Summary
Case report presenting what the authors describe as "the first case of human-reported froth in the respiratory tract following electrocution" — a finding that mimics drowning.

Case: A male construction worker died following alleged electrocution from an electric cutter. No external injuries associated with electric shock were identified at autopsy. The distinctive finding was whitish-red foam draining from nasal passages, with lungs showing a frothy, whitish-red appearance extending into the bronchioles.

Pathophysiology proposed: Electrocution destabilizes the pulmonary autonomic nervous system, leading to pulmonary vein constriction, rising pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure, alveolar wall damage, fluid leakage into intra-alveolar and interstitial regions, and bleeding — resulting in frothing.

Clinical significance: Frothing may serve as a diagnostic indicator when typical electrical burn marks are absent, though other causes (including drowning) must be excluded first.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: Yes — one occupational electrocution case (not in water, but forensically relevant)
- Prevalence estimates: None
- Forensic markers: Whitish-red foam from nasal passages and bronchioles; absent external electrical marks; pulmonary autonomic dysfunction leading to frothy pulmonary edema. CRITICAL NOTE: This finding can mimic drowning appearance, adding diagnostic complexity for ESD cases
- Key researchers: Thakral S, Aeron S, Setia P (MBBS, MD, DNB — likely Indian forensic pathology academics based on degree designations)

## Notes
This paper is doubly relevant to ESD research: (1) It documents that electrocution can cause frothy pulmonary edema that looks like drowning even without water immersion, and (2) conversely, when a drowning victim is also electrocuted, frothing from the electrocution component may be misattributed entirely to the drowning. The finding that electrocution without water can produce froth that mimics drowning is critical for understanding how ESD deaths may be diagnosed purely as drowning.
