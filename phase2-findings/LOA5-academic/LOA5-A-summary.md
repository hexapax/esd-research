## Executive Summary

The peer-reviewed medical and forensic literature on Electric Shock Drowning (ESD) is sparse but coherent. No papers were found in JAMA, NEJM, Lancet, or Annals of Emergency Medicine specifically on ESD. The primary literature consists of:

1. Forensic pathology papers on diagnosing electrocution in wet/immersion settings (American, German, and Australian authors)
2. A single CDC MMWR report documenting lake deaths in Oklahoma (1995/1996)
3. IEEE engineering papers quantifying ESD risk and water conductivity (2019-2020)
4. ASME risk engineering papers on ESD in marina settings (2016)
5. A 2024 systematic review of "unusual cause" drownings that includes ESD

**Central finding of the literature:** ESD deaths are diagnostically indistinguishable from ordinary drowning at autopsy because water prevents the formation of electrical burn marks. This is confirmed by multiple independent forensic pathology studies. The literature universally identifies scene/electrical investigation as the only reliable way to establish ESD as cause of death.

## Key Findings by Theme

### 1. Forensic Diagnosis of ESD

The consistent finding across all forensic pathology literature is that:

**Water eliminates electrical burn marks.** Karger et al. (ACAD-004) found that 100% of 7 wet-environment electrocution cases lacked electrical burns, even though 81% of all electrocution cases showed burns. Goodson (ACAD-001) established this as the fundamental challenge: the cooling effect of water prevents tissue heating. Byard (ACAD-009) calls cases where "electrocution is evident from scene findings but no injuries appear at autopsy" a major evaluation problem.

**No specific internal markers exist.** Mansueto et al. (ACAD-005) reviewed all known autopsy markers and concluded that internal organ findings "are still unspecific" for electrocution diagnosis. The electrical skin mark is the "golden standard" — but water prevents its formation.

**Petechiae are the best secondary finding.** Karger et al. (ACAD-004) found petechial hemorrhages in 74% of electrocution cases, including wet-environment cases. These are at eyelids, conjunctivae, visceral pleura, and epicardium. Not caused by asphyxia per se but by cardiac arrest plus blood pressure changes.

**Frothing may indicate electrocution.** Thakral et al. (ACAD-010) document the first reported case of frothy pulmonary edema from electrocution alone (no water). This confounds ESD diagnosis: in ESD deaths, frothing attributed to drowning may actually be partly caused by electrocution's pulmonary effects.

**Scene investigation is the only reliable diagnostic method.** All papers agree that without electrical scene evidence (faulty wiring, elevated voltage readings, identified current path), ESD cannot be confirmed at autopsy.

### 2. Epidemiology and Underreporting

**No national ESD-specific statistics exist.** The CDC does not separately categorize or track ESD deaths. ICD-10 codes used for drowning surveillance (W65-W74, V90, V92) do not distinguish ESD from other drownings.

**The only documented U.S. cluster:** The Oklahoma MMWR report (ACAD-002) documents 5 deaths over ~4 years at two lake systems. An inspection found 96% of 116 commercial docks at one lake violated the NEC. This is the only published federal surveillance data on ESD clusters at marinas/lakes.

**Industry/advocacy estimates:** The Electric Shock Drowning Prevention Association (ESDPA) tracks at least 60 confirmed ESD deaths since they began monitoring; the actual number is believed to be much higher due to misclassification. The IAEI 2007 article (ACAD-012) references a compiled catalog of 100+ similar accidents. These are not peer-reviewed statistics.

**What fraction of "drowning" deaths are ESD?** No peer-reviewed paper provides an estimate. The literature consistently notes that the fraction is unknown and likely underestimated due to the diagnostic challenges described above.

### 3. ESD Physics and Risk

**ESD occurs across all water types, not just freshwater.** Morse et al. (ACAD-007) demonstrate via FEM simulation that ESD risk exists in fresh, salt, and pool water. The freshwater focus in public guidance may reflect the prevalence of freshwater marinas rather than a true physics restriction.

**Low voltage is sufficient.** As little as 2 V/ft gradient can be lethal (ACAD-012 Shafer); 10 mA can cause paralysis in children.

**Quantitative risk methodology exists.** Ayyub et al. (ACAD-008) provide a formal risk assessment framework for marina/houseboat ESD risk. Companion simulation paper (Koko et al.) provides numerical scenarios.

## Gaps Identified

1. **No epidemiological studies** quantifying ESD deaths as a fraction of all drowning deaths
2. **No clinical outcome studies** of ESD survivors — treatment protocols are extrapolated from general electrocution management
3. **No systematic review** of ESD forensic diagnosis methods specifically (the DIPUCs paper, ACAD-006, is the closest)
4. **No national registry** or surveillance system tracks ESD separately from drowning
5. **No papers** from JAMA, NEJM, Lancet, or Annals of Emergency Medicine on ESD
6. **No papers** on the legal/statistical misclassification problem (lack of ICD-10 code for ESD)
7. **Limited data on non-U.S. ESD** — European, Australian, Asian literature almost entirely absent
8. **The MMWR report (ACAD-002) from 1996 remains the only CDC surveillance publication** on this topic — no follow-up federal surveillance published in 30 years
