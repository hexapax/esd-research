## Abstract/Summary
Comprehensive review of pathological markers for diagnosing electrocution at autopsy. Key findings:

The electrical mark is identified as the "golden standard for diagnosis of electrocution," particularly when other definitive evidence is absent.

Skin findings on histological examination:
- Dermal-epidermal detachment at variable depths
- Nuclear elongation with hyperchromasia
- Coagulative necrosis of the epidermis
- Collagen homogenization in superficial dermis (especially at high voltage)
- Higher voltages (≥48V) produce more extensive, continuous dermo-epidermal detachment

Internal organ findings (noted as non-specific):
- Heart: focal myocardial necrosis, interstitial hemorrhage, myofiber fragmentation with square nuclei
- Brain: hemorrhage, edema, ischemic changes, axonal degeneration
- Lungs: pulmonary edema and congestion secondary to cardiac dysfunction
- Muscles: rhabdomyolysis with fiber fragmentation and necrosis

Critical limitation: "organ histological changes are undoubtedly the starting point for understanding," yet internal organ pathology "are still unspecific" for certain electrocution diagnosis without supporting circumstances and skin evidence.

Published as volume 11(4):682.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: No — review article
- Prevalence estimates: None
- Forensic markers: Electrical mark as gold standard; skin histology with nuclear elongation, coagulative necrosis, dermo-epidermal detachment; internal findings non-specific; diagnosis requires scene evidence when marks absent
- Key researchers: Campobasso CP (corresponding author); multiple Italian authors affiliated with University of Naples and related institutions

## Notes
This review is directly relevant to ESD forensics because it establishes the fundamental challenge: without an electrical mark (which water prevents forming, per ACAD-001 and ACAD-004), there is no pathological gold standard for diagnosing electrocution at autopsy. This paper effectively explains at the cellular/histological level why ESD is so difficult to diagnose and why site investigation is indispensable. The authors' finding that all internal findings are "non-specific" validates the claim that ESD deaths are routinely misclassified as simple drowning.
