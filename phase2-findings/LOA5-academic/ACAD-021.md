## Key Technical Content

Linja-aho's discussion paper responds to Morse et al. (ACAD-020) and provides a comparative international perspective. Key contributions:

- Analysis of 157 fatal electrical accidents in Finland from 1980–2019 found zero ESD incidents despite Finland having abundant freshwater lakes and significant marina/boating activity
- Attributes absence of ESD fatalities in Finland primarily to mandatory residual current device (RCD) protection, which has been required for all shore-power connections in Europe for approximately 30 years
- Notes that national differences in accident types are explained by regulatory environment, not by physics alone: the US has not uniformly mandated ELCI/RCD protection for shore power, while Europe has
- Argues that the risk of ESD in non-US freshwater environments is real but effectively mitigated by RCD requirements, supporting the view that 30 mA RCDs/ELCIs are the critical protective technology
- Provides context that ESD incidents appear "completely inexistent in Finland" despite identical freshwater physics — demonstrating that the hazard is preventable

## ESD Incidents Cited

No specific incidents cited. References Finnish fatal electrical accident database showing no ESD cases in 40-year period.

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

Provides strong international evidence that mandatory RCD/ELCI requirements (as Europe has implemented) effectively eliminate ESD fatalities. This supports advocacy for mandatory ELCI/GFPE requirements in US marina regulations (NEC Article 555, NFPA 303). Linja-aho's companion paper on Finnish fatal electrical accidents (ACAD-022) provides the underlying data.

## Relevance Rating

HIGH — Provides peer-reviewed international comparative evidence that ESD is preventable through RCD requirements. Directly relevant to the regulatory debate about why ESD occurs in the US but not in European countries with identical freshwater physics.
