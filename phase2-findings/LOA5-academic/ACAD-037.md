## Key Technical Content

Canadian electrical inspector perspective on ESD in swimming pools specifically, with comparison of US and Canadian regulatory regimes:

- Defines ESD mechanism: "low-level AC current through the body with sufficient force causes skeletal muscle paralysis" in fresh water, leading to drowning
- Explains the physiological basis: skin resistance drops drastically when wet (from ~20,000 ohms dry to dramatically lower when wet), and the human body being ~70% water makes it conductive relative to freshwater
- **US regulations applicable to pools:**
  - NEC Article 680 (pools, spas, hot tubs)
  - NEC Article 555 (marinas, updated 2020)
  - NFPA 303 and NFPA 70
- **Canadian regulations:**
  - Canadian Electrical Code (CEC) Part I, Section 78 (swimming pools)
  - Rule 78-050: Class A GFCI (5 mA) protection required
  - CSA C22.2 No. 108 (pump standards)
- **Protection thresholds compared:**
  - US: GFPE 30 mA at receptacles; 100 mA at main marina supply (NEC Article 555.35)
  - Canada: Class A GFCI at 5 mA (more protective than US 30 mA GFPE threshold)
- Distance requirements: Up to 8 meters (Canadian CEC) clearance between conductors/luminaires and pool areas
- **CPSC data cited**: 33 people died and 33 were injured from pool electrocution between 2002 and 2018 (US Consumer Product Safety Commission)

## ESD Incidents Cited

References CPSC data (33 deaths, 33 injuries in pools 2002–2018). Does not cite specific named incidents.

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

Provides comparative US-Canadian regulatory analysis. Notes Canada's 5 mA Class A GFCI requirement as more protective than US 30 mA threshold for marina GFPE. Relevant to Canadian provinces adopting marina safety standards. Shows that pool electrocutions (which share physics with ESD in marinas) are tracked by CPSC and occur at the rate of approximately 2 deaths per year in documented pool incidents.

## Relevance Rating

MODERATE — Important for the regulatory comparison between US and Canadian standards. The CPSC pool electrocution data (33 deaths 2002–2018) contextualizes marina ESD within the broader freshwater electrocution problem. The Canadian 5 mA threshold comparison is relevant to debates about strengthening US protection requirements.
