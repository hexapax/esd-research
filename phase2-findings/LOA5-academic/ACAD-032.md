## Key Technical Content

Written by a professional engineer, this IAEI article provides an authoritative electrical inspector's perspective on marina safety and ESD:

- Traces the history of NEC Article 555 from its 1968 origin as "Boat Harbor Wiring" through 2020 restructuring
- Documents the tiered approach in 2020 NEC: 100 mA GFPE for feeders, 30 mA GFPE for shore power receptacles, Class A GFCI (4–6 mA) for general outlets
- Explains why the 100 mA threshold (from 2011 NEC) was insufficient: "far above Class A GFCI levels of 4–6 mA" and could not prevent muscle tetanization in children
- Discusses NFPA 303 requirements that complement NEC Article 555 for marina facility management
- Notes the collaborative process: "working together was the best option as everyone...agreed that lives were at stake"
- Describes specific hazards: movement of floating docks can damage wiring; tidal/wave action creates intermittent faults; shore power cords deteriorate; boat wiring may be non-code-compliant
- Discusses leakage current measurement requirements for marinas with more than three shore power receptacles

**Lucas Ritz case**: The article specifically references Lucas, an 8-year-old (actually: article says "eight-year-old," though ESDPA data says Lucas Ritz was 10 at time of 1999 death) who died near a boat dock after becoming immobilized by electrical current while swimming.

## ESD Incidents Cited

- Lucas Ritz case (1999): child died swimming near boat dock, became immobilized by electrical current without touching any conductive material

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

IAEI Magazine is read by electrical inspectors and code officials throughout the US and Canada. This article explains the regulatory rationale for NEC Article 555 changes in terms accessible to inspectors implementing the code. Supports the case for regular marina electrical inspections.
