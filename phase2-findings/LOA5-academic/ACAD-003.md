# ACAD-003: An Unusual Case of Homicidal Near Drowning Followed by Electrocution

## Abstract/Summary
A forensic case report involving a young woman discovered in a bathtub with facial orifices submerged and current burn marks on her right big toe and neck. The initial hypothesis was accidental electrocution from a radio. Comprehensive forensic analysis — including histology, immunohistochemistry, and energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy — revealed a deliberate two-stage attack. Findings indicated "intensive vital reactions of a near-drowning" with "a survival period of at least 20 min," followed by deliberate application of electrical current causing cardiac arrest. This established the case as homicide.

Published as volume 120(1), pages 36-41.

## Relevance to ESD Research
- Incidents documented: Yes — one case (bathtub setting, homicidal), demonstrates sequential near-drowning then electrocution
- Prevalence estimates: None — single case report
- Forensic markers: Current burn marks (right big toe and neck); histology and immunohistochemistry showing vital near-drowning reactions; energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy for confirming electrical marks; survival period of at least 20 minutes post-near-drowning before fatal shock
- Key researchers: H. Pfeiffer, A. Du Chesne, B. Brinkmann (Institut fur Rechtsmedizin, Germany)

## Notes
While this case involves a bathtub rather than open water or marina setting, it is highly relevant to ESD forensics because it demonstrates: (1) the forensic toolkit needed to distinguish between drowning and electrocution — and their sequencing, (2) that water immersion can precede electrocution rather than follow it, and (3) that histology and immunohistochemistry can establish timing and vital reactions. The techniques described are directly applicable to ESD death investigations. B. Brinkmann is a prominent forensic pathologist whose broader work on electrical deaths is foundational to the field.
