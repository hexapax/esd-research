## Key Technical Content

Practitioner-focused technical guide on stray current testing protocols for marina operators, covering both AC (ESD hazard) and DC (corrosion hazard) stray currents:

**Classification of stray currents:**
- AC stray currents: Greatest risk to human life; caused by AC power source grounding schemes from marina wiring, boat wiring, and utility wiring
- DC stray currents: Greatest risk to underwater metals; includes electrolytic DC and galvanic currents

**AC fault current testing protocol:**
1. Verify proper polarity at all receptacles
2. Use clamp-on milliampere meters to measure fault current with circuit breakers on
3. Measure currents on individual conductors when possible
4. Measure with circuit breakers off
5. Energize boat loads one circuit at a time while monitoring for fault current increases

**Critical finding on detection limitations**: Fault current flowing through ground conductors will NOT trip dock receptacle ground-fault protection or boat ELCI protection. If neutral is connected to ground at multiple points (a wiring fault), neutral current flows through ground conductors, creating hazardous conditions that evade standard protection devices.

**DC stray current testing**: Separate protocols for galvanic and electrolytic DC currents; requires reference electrode measurements and hull potential surveys

**Practical tools**: Clamp-on milliammeter; multimeter; reference electrodes for DC work; oscilloscope for AC/DC distinction

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

IMBC presentations are the primary professional development resource for marina operators and managers. This testing protocol directly supports the annual inspection requirements in NFPA 303 and NEC Article 555. The AMI published this as a reference document for its membership.

## Relevance Rating

HIGH — Provides the practical testing protocol that marina operators should use to detect ESD hazards. The insight about fault current bypassing GFCI/ELCI protection (consistent with the Dolan/Norton/Loftis paper, ACAD-034) is particularly important for understanding why protection devices alone are insufficient without testing.
