## Key Technical Content

This IEEE guide addresses voltage conditions at publicly and privately accessible locations, with explicit mention of marinas, boat docks, and swimming pools as environments of concern. Key provisions:

- Distinguishes "stray voltage" (from normal electrical system operation, no fault) from "contact voltage" (from an electrical fault, requires repair)
- Provides investigation and measurement techniques for identifying the source of accessible voltages
- Recommends 500-ohm resistor for field measurements to represent combined body, skin, and contact resistance (simulating human body impedance under wet conditions)
- Provides Contact Voltage Detection (CVD) program guidance applicable to marina environments
- Addresses detection thresholds: voltage level alone is not sufficient to distinguish stray voltage from contact voltage — current path and source must be determined
- Covers human and animal electrical sensitivity, investigation equipment, and remedial actions
- Marina-specific application: use of point-to-point voltage measurements between water and reference ground point to detect energized water conditions

Updated version IEEE 1695-2024 is under development with reorganization and expanded case studies including aquatic environments.

## ESD Incidents Cited

Guide does not cite specific ESD incidents. Mentions marinas and boat docks as application environments for the measurement and mitigation techniques described.

## Regulatory/Legislative Impact

IEEE 1695-2016 is referenced in training materials for marina electrical inspectors and ESD investigators. Complements NEC Article 555 and NFPA 303 by providing measurement methodology. Referenced in EPRI stray voltage workshop presentations (2023) and used by utility companies investigating voltage complaints in marina environments.

## Relevance Rating

MODERATE — This standard provides the measurement methodology for detecting stray and contact voltage in marina environments. Important reference for ESD investigators and marina electrical inspectors, but is not specifically written for ESD and does not address the specific physics of current flow through water swimmers.
