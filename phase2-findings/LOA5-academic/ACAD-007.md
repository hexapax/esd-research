| Field | Value |

| **Journal** | IEEE Transactions on Industry Applications |
| **Year** | 2020 |

## Abstract/Summary
This paper challenges the widely held belief that ESD can only occur in freshwater environments. Using Finite Element Method (FEM) simulations of conductivity ranging from 0.005 S/m (pure water) to 4.8 S/m (seawater), the authors demonstrate that ESD risk exists across a broad range of water conductivities, not just in freshwater.

Key findings:
- Common belief that ESD is limited to freshwater (because current shunts around the body in saltwater) is challenged by simulation results
- ESD drowning can occur in fresh water, salt water, and pool water (conductivity ~2.5-3.0 S/m)
- Simulations define a "Zone of Danger" for swimmer body current exposure as a function of water conductivity, source placement, and ground placement
- Lead author Dr. Morse has personally evaluated several cases in which individuals in fresh, salt, and chlorinated water have died from electrical current flow, from either electrically induced ventricular fibrillation or electrically induced muscle paralysis
- Suggests using sensors to monitor current in water near docks rather than relying solely on protective devices

Published as volume 56, issue 4, pages 4324-4328. Originally presented at 2019 IEEE IAS Electrical Safety Workshop, Jacksonville (DOI: 10.1109/ESW41045.2019.9024720).

Authors: Michael S. Morse and James G. Kohl are professors at the University of San Diego. A discussion paper by Vesa Linja-aho was published in response (IEEE Transactions on Industry Applications, Vol. 57, No. 1, Jan/Feb 2021).

## Notes
This is the primary peer-reviewed engineering/physics paper on ESD risk quantification. Its finding that ESD can occur in salt and pool water — not just freshwater — has significant implications for how broadly ESD is defined and how many deaths might be attributable to it. The companion discussion paper by Linja-aho (2021) provides further analysis. The EC&M Magazine article "The Science Behind Electric Shock Drowning" (November 2018) by Morse gives accessible background. Morse's publication list at electricalinjury.com documents his broader work on electrical injury.
