## Narrative

At approximately 4:00 p.m. on July 21, 2016, Employee #1 (age 24) was installing tongue-and-groove wood trim on the ceiling of a new dock area under construction. During this work, he came into contact with an energized source at the boat lift. He received an electric shock and fell into the water. Employee #1 drowned.

OSHA keywords: Drowning, Electrical, Shock.

## Analysis

**This is a confirmed ESD-mechanism fatality in an occupational context.** The victim was a construction worker installing trim at a newly constructed dock facility. He contacted an energized boat lift — boat lifts are a documented common source of ESD-mechanism current in water. After receiving the shock, he fell into the water and drowned. This is precisely the ESD mechanism: electrical current from dock infrastructure (here a boat lift) incapacitates a person who then drowns.

The distinction from recreational ESD is that the victim was a worker rather than a swimmer, and his primary contact with the electrical source was direct (touching the energized boat lift structure) rather than through stray current diffusing in the water. However, his death — falling into water after electric shock, drowning — is mechanistically identical to ESD.

**Why not in ESDPA:** ESDPA documents recreational ESD victims. OSHA records occupational fatalities. This incident falls in OSHA's jurisdiction. The boat lift as an energized source in a dock construction context is directly relevant to understanding how dock electrical systems create ESD hazards.

**Construction industry note:** NAICS 236118 covers construction contractors, not marina operators. This means the victim was a construction worker employed by LHC Construction Management, LLC, working at a new dock/marina facility. The hazardous energized boat lift would have been either newly installed infrastructure or existing marina equipment that was still energized during construction — a recognized hazard requiring lockout/tagout procedures.
