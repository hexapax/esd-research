## Narrative

On June 13, 2008, at approximately unknown time, an employee of Coastal Carolina Construction & Development, Inc. was performing repair work on a floating dock at Ocean Isle Beach Marina. He was using an electric drill to drill holes into the dock to install cross bracing. The employee had his feet in the water while operating the electric drill, which was plugged into a marina receptacle. The receptacle was not protected by a ground fault circuit interrupter (GFCI). The employee was electrocuted and transported to Brunswick Community Hospital, where he was pronounced dead on arrival.

OSHA keywords from investigation record: ELECTROCUTED, CONSTRUCTION, GROUND FAULT, ELECTRIC DRILL, WATER.

## Analysis

**This is a confirmed ESD-adjacent occupational fatality.** The mechanism is direct: a construction worker with feet in water operated an energized power tool connected to an unprotected (no GFCI) marina receptacle. The lack of GFCI is the direct cause — had GFCI protection been in place on the marina receptacle, the ground fault created when current entered the water through the worker would have tripped the breaker. The receptacle was the product of marina electrical infrastructure, not the drill itself.

**Why not in ESDPA:** ESDPA focuses on recreational victims in water near energized dock/marina infrastructure. This victim was a construction worker actively operating a defective (ungrounded per GFCI) electrical tool system. The immediate hazard was the tool/receptacle combination, not stray current in the water affecting a swimmer. However, the OSHA citation for 29 CFR 1926.404(B)(1)(ii) (GFCI in wet conditions) is directly relevant to dock/marina electrical safety standards.

**OSHA jurisdiction confirmed:** Construction workers repairing a floating dock are OSHA-covered workers. Marina infrastructure electrical defects (lack of GFCI on receptacles) created the fatal hazard. This is one of the clearest OSHA-documented cases of marina electrical infrastructure causing a fatality.
